Customer Journey Framework

Two personas, one agent. Maya Torres (CX Operations Manager) sets up and refines the agent. Daniel Reyes (GM of E-Commerce) sees the bigger picture — support and marketing unified into a single customer experience. The vision: as the agent matures, the ICP shifts from CX manager to GM of ecommerce.

🌸 The Merchant

UrbanStems — Premium flower & gifting brand. Seasonal peaks (Valentine's, Mother's Day), growing subscription base ("BloomPlan"), perishable products with 2-day freshness guarantee.

  • ~4,200 tickets/month, 61% automated
  • BloomPlan: $52/delivery, biweekly
  • Mother's Day: 3.5× normal volume
  • AOV: $68 (one-time), $52 (subscription)

👤 Maya Torres

CX Operations Manager — Protagonist, Journeys 1-2. Former senior support agent, moved into ops 6 months ago when UrbanStems adopted Gorgias AI Agent. Thinks in customer outcomes, not code.

  • Goal: push automation from 61% → 80% before Mother's Day
  • Manages 3 human agents; owns escalation quality
  • Cares about: agent accuracy, team workload, deflection

👔 Daniel Reyes

GM of E-Commerce — Protagonist, Journeys 3-4. Oversees full customer lifecycle: acquisition, conversion, support, retention. Reports to CEO. Pays attention when the agent crosses from support into revenue.

  • Evaluating: can Gorgias be the platform for the entire customer lifecycle?
  • Cares about: conversion rate, customer LTV, retention, unified experience
  • Key insight: support and marketing are both skills the agent can use at the right moment

Journey 1 Mac / Lisa

Onboarding — "Getting the Agent Ready"

Maya sets up the AI agent through conversation. She activates skills she trusts, leaves the high-stakes ones off for now, and calibrates the brand voice.

1

The agent greets Maya — and already knows her business

UrbanStems just migrated from Zendesk. The agent has already ingested their historical ticket data — 14 months of conversations. Instead of asking Maya to describe her business from scratch, it leads with what it's learned: "I've analyzed 58,000 tickets from your Zendesk history. Here's what your customers write about most."

2

The agent proposes skills based on real ticket data

From the Zendesk history, the agent has identified the top intents and their volume. It proposes 6 skills mapped directly to what customers actually ask about — not generic templates, but recommendations grounded in UrbanStems' real conversation patterns.

3

Maya activates selectively

She turns on 3 skills she's confident about (Order Tracking, Delivery Issues, Gift Messaging) and leaves Subscription Management and Return & Refund off for now. She wants to see the agent handle simpler stuff before trusting it with cancellations.

4

Brand voice calibration

Maya describes the tone: "Warm but not cutesy. We send flowers for funerals too, not just birthdays. Empathetic, concise, never robotic." The agent generates 3 sample responses to a delivery delay for her to pick from and edit.

5

Policy ingestion

Maya pastes their refund policy. The agent summarizes it back in plain language and asks for confirmation. Maya adjusts the damage-claim photo threshold from $75 to $60.

6

Go live

The agent starts handling tickets. "I'll send you a digest tomorrow morning with how the first 24 hours went. If anything looks off, just tell me here."

Journey 2 Ines / Chris

Daily Digest → Inbox → Automation

Maya's morning routine. The digest surfaces insights from overnight conversations. She drops into the inbox to handle what the agent couldn't — and by the end, the agent has learned a new skill from watching how she resolved it.

1

Maya opens the daily digest — 8:47 AM, Week 3

The agent has been live for two weeks. The digest greets her with a contextual summary and four metric cards. "Customer satisfaction is up to 4.3 — the brand voice tweaks you made Friday are landing well. Adherence holding steady at 88%."

2

Two insight cards demand attention

First: a seed — "5 of 14 cancellations yesterday mentioned delivery frequency. I'm tracking this." (This pays off in Journey 4.) Second: 4 escalated tickets the agent couldn't handle — all about pet safety.

3

Maya drops into the inbox — and spots a problem

3 of the 4 pet safety tickets were escalated correctly — the agent said "I'm not sure, let me connect you with our team." But the 4th one is bad. The agent confidently told Marcus Webb that the Sunset Lily arrangement is "perfectly safe for dogs." Lilies are toxic to cats and can cause kidney issues in dogs. The agent didn't know this — it had no pet safety data — and guessed wrong instead of escalating.

4

Maya fixes the damage and sees the pattern

She immediately messages Marcus with a correction and offers to swap to a pet-safe arrangement at no charge. Then she resolves the other 3. The pattern is clear: check if the bouquet contains toxic flowers, suggest a pet-safe alternative. But the failure makes the fix urgent — without pet safety knowledge, the agent will keep guessing, and the next guess might not get caught.

5

The agent proposes a proactive skill update

"These 4 customers caught the issue after ordering — but how many didn't? If I proactively ask gift buyers whether the recipient has pets, I can recommend pet-safe arrangements before the order goes through. No more toxic bouquets arriving at homes with cats." Maya reviews the proposed update and activates it.

Part 1: The Insight

The agent surfaces a pattern it found by reading cancellation conversations at scale.

1

Post-peak analysis — Week 7: Daniel's Dashboard

Mother's Day is over. Daniel reviews his commerce dashboard. Acquisition was strong (3.5× normal volume). But he notices something: churn spiked in the first week post-peak. The agent surfaces why: 35% of cancellations mention frequency language — "too many," "can't keep up." This is a retention problem neither his marketing team nor the support team saw separately. The unified insight: support conversations contain revenue signals.

2

The agent shows the data

35% of cancellations (31 of 89) contained frequency language — "too many," "can't keep up," "every two weeks is too much." These are customers who love the product but can't commit to the cadence. They're not a support problem. They're a retention problem — addressable through a simple schedule adjustment. Current save rate: 0%. Daniel sees the potential: if frequency detection and schedule adjustment save even 30% of these, that's ~$416/mo in recurring revenue. Not a support efficiency gain. A revenue lever.

3

Example tickets with highlighted language

The agent shows 3 real conversations where frequency language appears. Maya can expand each to see the full exchange and what the agent actually responded.

✦

Part 2: Offline Evaluation — Testing at Scale

Daniel approves a test. Before risking real customers, the agent validates the hypothesis at scale using historical data.

4

The agent proposes a skill change

"I want to add a frequency detection step. When I detect frequency language, I'd offer to adjust the schedule (every 3 weeks, monthly) before processing the cancellation. Before we put this in front of real customers, I can test it against historical tickets."

5

847 historical tickets evaluated

The agent replays every subscription conversation from the past 3 months through the new instructions. Adherence: 94%. 312 correctly identified as frequency-related. 486 correctly identified as standard cancellations. 49 edge cases flagged.

6

Maya reviews edge cases

The agent surfaces the 3 riskiest: "too much" meaning price not frequency, a customer already offered a schedule change last month, and "too many emails" (marketing, not deliveries). Maya adjusts the instructions. The agent re-runs — adherence improves to 97%, edge cases drop from 49 to 12.

✦

Part 3: A/B Test — Real-World Validation

Offline eval looks solid. Now the change goes to real traffic.

7

Custom scorers — Maya adds qualitative evaluation

Daniel sits in with Maya. He pushes for a business-focused scorer: "I don't just care if the agent can detect frequency. I care if the retention works — and more importantly, that the customer feels heard, not strong-armed." The team creates custom scorers: Tone (helpful suggestion, not a retention trap), Accuracy (only flag real frequency, not price/quality confusion), and a new one: Sentiment (is the customer's tone positive post-interaction, even if they choose a different frequency or cancel?). This is Daniel's criteria — the agent should be building lifetime value, not just save rate.

8

A/B test launched

Variant A: frequency-aware (detect signals, offer schedule adjustment). Variant B: current skill (confirm and cancel). 50/50 split, 3 weeks. Standard metrics + Maya's custom scorers running on every conversation.

9

Results: Variant A wins

Coverage stable. Adherence at 96%. But the metrics Daniel cares about: 8 subscriptions saved out of 27 frequency-identified customers = $416/mo in recurring revenue retained. Annualized: ~$5,000 saved. Customer LTV for saved subscribers improved. Churn rate for frequency cohort down 6%. This is what unified commerce intelligence looks like — support conversations generating revenue insights and outcomes.

10

Agent self-critique

"3 conversations were misclassified. One customer said 'too many wilted flowers' — that's a quality complaint, not a frequency one. I need better signal detection to distinguish 'too many deliveries' from 'too many problems.' Want me to propose a follow-up experiment?" Daniel is impressed. The agent isn't just executing — it's self-correcting, learning, proposing better iterations. This is how products scale. He starts thinking: what else spans support and revenue? Returns drive product quality feedback. Support conversations inform marketing segmentation. One agent, reading the full context, driving the whole funnel.

Custom Scorers

Beyond standard metrics, Maya defines qualitative criteria the agent should evaluate on every conversation during the experiment. These run as automated assessments alongside adherence, success rate, and coverage.

Custom Scorer 1

Tone — helpful suggestion, not a save gate

When offering a schedule adjustment, the agent should feel like it's genuinely helping, not blocking the cancellation. The customer should always feel they can say "no" and cancel immediately without friction.

Custom Scorer 2

Accuracy — only flag real frequency complaints

The agent should only trigger the frequency offer when the customer is actually unhappy with cadence. "Too much" about price, quality, or emails should not trigger the offer. Misclassifications = bad experience.

How custom scorers work

The agent evaluates each conversation against Maya's criteria and scores it pass/fail. In the results, Maya can see: "Tone scorer: 94% pass — 2 conversations felt slightly pushy when the customer hesitated." and "Accuracy scorer: 91% pass — 3 conversations triggered on non-frequency language." This gives her qualitative insight alongside the quantitative metrics.
